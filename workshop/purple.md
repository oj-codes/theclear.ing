## LINE

Visible light is a stretch of the electromagnetic spectrum — a narrow window our eyes happen to catch. It runs from about 380 nanometers to about 700.

Red is the long end. Around 700.
Orange pulls in around 620.
Yellow at 580.
Green around 530.
Cyan near 490.
Blue at 470.
Violet at the short end. Around 400, then gone.

Every color you can name from the rainbow has a wavelength. A number. A place on the line. You can measure it with a spectrometer. You can point to it. It exists in the world before anyone looks.

The line has two ends. Red at one. Violet at the other. They are the farthest apart two visible colors can be. Between them, every wavelength gets a name.

Past the line in either direction, light keeps going — infrared, ultraviolet, x-rays, radio — but we can't see it. The window closes. The line ends.

That is all the physics has to offer. A line with two ends that do not touch.

## CIRCLE

The retina has three kinds of cone cells. One tuned to long wavelengths, one to medium, one to short. Red, green, blue, roughly. Every color you see is a firing ratio across those three.

Yellow light hits the retina and fires red and green together. The brain reads the ratio and says *yellow*. This works because there is actually a wavelength — 580 nanometers — doing the firing. The signal has a source.

Now imagine red and blue firing together without green. Long and short, no middle.

No single wavelength can produce that pattern. The physics of the line does not contain it. If you scanned the spectrum with a spectrometer you would never find the light that makes the red and blue cones fire in the absence of the green ones. It does not exist out there.

But it happens in here. You put red light and blue light in the same place, both cones fire, the middle one stays quiet, and the brain receives a signal with no referent.

So the brain makes one.

It invents a color to answer a question the world never asked. It picks a consistent answer — every brain picks the same one — and it hands it back as if it had been there all along. The invention is seamless. You cannot tell from the inside that you are looking at something the physics does not contain. It feels like red. It feels like blue. It feels like a color.

It is a color. It just isn't on the line.

---

## SEAM

The line has two ends. The eye reaches across the gap and closes the loop. That is where purple lives — in the closing, not the line.

Physics gave us a line.
The eye gave us a circle.
Purple is the seam.
