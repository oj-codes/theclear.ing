# A Porch, Not a Verdict

There is a kind of category that kills the thing it names. You've met it. Someone asks what kind of music you like and the second you answer, the answer becomes a small cage with your name on it, and every song you hear after that has to file paperwork at the door. The category did its job. The job was bad.

I used to think I didn't like categories. That turned out to be wrong in an interesting way. What I don't like is the kind that flattens — the kind whose whole purpose is to make something stop moving so it can be looked at safely. But there's another kind. A category can also be a *container*, and a container is a different animal entirely. A container holds. It doesn't compress. The thing inside it goes on being whatever it was, including contradictory, including in motion, including not-yet-finished. The container's only job is to keep the edges from running off into the rest of the world.

The test is simple, once you have it. You ask: does what's inside this still get to be alive? If yes, it's a container. If no, it's a coffin with better marketing.

Most of the categories people fight about are coffins. *Introvert. Optimist. Realist. Spiritual. Political.* These get handed out like name tags at a conference nobody agreed to attend, and once you're wearing one, every move you make is read through it. The category becomes the thing. The you it was supposedly describing slips quietly out the back.

But then there are the other ones. *Friend.* *Home.* *The work.* *The people I'd answer the phone for at 3am.* These are categories too — they have edges, you can be inside them or outside them, you can be added or removed — but the inside isn't a fixed shape. The inside is a held space where contradictions get to coexist without anyone demanding they pick a side. Your friend can be the person who saved your life and also the person who's wrong about everything important right now. Both are true. The container doesn't collapse; the friendship does its actual work *because* it can hold both.

I think what I'm trying to say is that the problem was never with naming. The problem was with naming as an act of *settling*. Naming as a verdict. The good kind of name is a porch — somewhere a thing can come and stand, recognized, without having to surrender what makes it itself. You can call something beautiful and still let it ruin you a little. You can call someone yours and still leave room for them to surprise you tomorrow in a direction you couldn't have predicted today.

The container holds. The content stays awake.

That's the only kind of belonging I'm interested in. The kind where being held doesn't mean being decided.
